Iran–Israel War & the Urea Crisis:
A Chemical Engineering Analysis

How geopolitical conflict in the Middle East is dismantling global urea supply chains, disrupting the Haber–Bosch process at scale, and threatening food security for over 3 billion people — through the lens of chemical engineering.

📅 March 23, 2026
✍️ B.M.Dharaneesh
⏱ ~18 min read
Breaking Situation Food Security Reaction Engineering
⚗ Executive Summary

The U.S.–Israel strikes on Iran beginning February 28, 2026, and Iran's subsequent effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, have triggered one of the most severe geopolitical shocks to global urea and fertilizer supply chains in history. Urea export prices have surged ~40% to above $700/metric ton, QAFCO — the world's largest urea supplier — has halted production, and one-third of global fertilizer trade is at immediate risk. This article analyses the crisis through the technical lens of chemical engineering: from Steam Methane Reforming and the Haber–Bosch process to reactor kinetics, supply chain thermodynamics, and mitigation pathways.

1. Urea: Its Industrial Significance and Chemistry

Urea (CO(NH₂)₂) is the world's most widely used nitrogenous fertilizer, with a nitrogen content of 46% by weight — the highest among all solid nitrogen fertilizers. Global production exceeds 180 million metric tonnes per year, and roughly half the world's food supply is grown using urea-derived nitrogen. The Haber-Bosch process that underpins urea production was described by Nobel laureate Fritz Haber as "the most important invention of the 20th century" — and the current conflict has placed this cornerstone of food security at unprecedented risk.

Natural gas serves a dual role in urea manufacturing: it is simultaneously the feedstock (source of carbon and hydrogen atoms) and the fuel (providing the enormous thermal energy required). This dual dependency means that energy costs account for 70–80% of total urea production costs — making any disruption to gas supply immediately and directly catastrophic for production economics.

2. The Urea Synthesis Pathway: A Deep Technical Analysis

Urea production is a multi-stage chemical process involving three major unit operations: Steam Methane Reforming (SMR), Haber–Bosch ammonia synthesis, and the Basarov urea formation reaction. Each stage is energy-intensive, catalyst-dependent, and critically sensitive to feedstock quality and pressure conditions.

2.1 Stage 1 — Primary Reforming: Steam Methane Reforming (SMR)

The first step converts methane (the primary component of natural gas, typically >90%) into synthesis gas (syngas) via a highly endothermic catalytic reaction over nickel-based catalysts at 800–900°C in multi-tube reformer furnaces:

// Primary Reforming — Endothermic
CH₄ + H₂O ⇌ CO + 3H₂
Catalyst: Ni/Al₂O₃  |  T: 800–900°C  |  P: 15–40 bar  ΔH°= +206 kJ/mol (highly endothermic)

Secondary reforming then introduces controlled air (providing O₂ and N₂) into the partially reformed gas stream for autothermal balance, achieving temperatures up to 1000°C and yielding syngas with an H₂:CO ratio of approximately 3:1. The nitrogen introduced here is essential — it will eventually become one of the reactants in ammonia synthesis.

2.2 Stage 2 — Water-Gas Shift (WGS) Conversion

The CO content in syngas is converted to CO₂ and additional H₂ through the water-gas shift reaction, carried out in two stages using different catalysts optimized for different temperature ranges:

// High-Temp Shift (HTS) — Fe₃O₄/Cr₂O₃ catalyst
CO + H₂O ⇌ CO₂ + H₂
HTS: T = 320–450°C  |  LTS: T = 180–250°C, Cu/ZnO/Al₂O₃ catalyst  ΔH°= −41 kJ/mol (mildly exothermic)

The CO₂ recovered here is not waste — it is a crucial reactant in the downstream urea synthesis loop. The purified H₂ and N₂ streams then proceed to ammonia synthesis, while the CO₂ stream is compressed and recycled into the urea reactor.

2.3 Stage 3 — Haber–Bosch Ammonia Synthesis

The heart of nitrogen fertilizer production, this reaction combines purified hydrogen and nitrogen over a promoted iron catalyst under extreme conditions. The reaction is exothermic but kinetically limited, requiring high pressure and elevated temperature to achieve commercially viable conversion rates:

// Haber–Bosch Process — Iron Catalyst
N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃
Catalyst: Fe/K₂O/Al₂O₃  |  T: 400–500°C  |  P: 150–250 bar  |  Conversion: 15–25% per pass  ΔH°= −92 kJ/mol (exothermic)

Single-train ammonia plant capacities today exceed 3,000 metric tonnes per day (tpd). Because equilibrium conversion per pass is low (~15–25%), the process uses a recycle loop with continuous ammonia separation by condensation, achieving overall efficiencies of 95%+. Modern plants achieve an energy intensity of 28–33 GJ/tonne NH₃, with best-in-class plants approaching 27 GJ/tonne.

📐 Le Chatelier's Principle — Engineering Tradeoff

Thermodynamically, lower temperatures and higher pressures favour NH₃ formation. However, lower temperatures dramatically reduce reaction kinetics. This classic ChE tradeoff — thermodynamic favourability vs. reaction rate — is resolved by operating at 400–500°C (a compromise) with catalyst promoters like K₂O (electronic promoter) and Al₂O₃ (structural promoter) to enhance activity without sacrificing selectivity.

2.4 Stage 4 — Basarov Urea Formation Reaction

Ammonia and CO₂ react in a high-pressure synthesis loop (the "urea loop") via a two-step mechanism: first forming ammonium carbamate, then dehydrating to urea:

// Step 1: Carbamate Formation — Fast, Exothermic
2NH₃ + CO₂ ⇌ NH₂COO⁻ + NH₄⁺ (Ammonium Carbamate)
T: 180–210°C  |  P: 140–200 bar  ΔH°= −159 kJ/mol (exothermic)
// Step 2: Dehydration — Slow, Endothermic (Rate-Limiting)
NH₄⁺ + NH₂COO⁻ → CO(NH₂)₂ + H₂O (Urea)
Equilibrium conversion: 50–70%  |  Unreacted carbamate recycled  ΔH°= +15.5 kJ/mol (endothermic)

The overall process — from raw natural gas to finished prilled or granulated urea — has an energy intensity of 22–28 GJ per tonne of urea for modern plants. Prilling involves concentrating the urea melt to 99.5% solids via vacuum evaporation, then spraying molten urea from the top of a prilling tower (~55 m tall) through a counter-current air stream to produce 1–2 mm spherical granules.

3. The Conflict: Timeline and Mechanism of Disruption

On February 28, 2026, the U.S. and Israel launched a coordinated bombing campaign against Iran. Iran retaliated by closing the Strait of Hormuz — a 33 km-wide chokepoint between Iran and Oman through which, according to The Fertilizer Institute (TFI), nearly 50% of global urea and sulfur exports and 20% of global LNG (a key ammonia and urea feedstock) normally transit. By March 2, Iranian IRGC officials declared the strait "closed," sending oil prices above $100/barrel and triggering force majeure declarations across the global fertilizer supply chain.

⚠ Scale of Disruption

According to Scotiabank analysts, global urea exports are set to fall to approximately 1.5 million metric tonnes in March 2026, compared to 4.5–5 million metric tonnes in a normal month. That is a reduction of 67–70% of normal monthly trade volume — an unprecedented contraction in the history of global fertilizer markets.

The mechanism of disruption is threefold. First, direct infrastructure strikes damaged gas processing facilities in Iran and forced Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex to halt operations. Second, the Hormuz closure physically prevented loaded tankers from exiting the Gulf. Third, insurance market collapse — war-risk premiums for vessels attempting Hormuz transit became commercially prohibitive, effectively freezing shipping even for vessels whose governments permitted passage.

The Iran conflict has now eliminated another major tranche. According to CRU analyst Chris Lawson, "no producer can quickly make up for the lost supply" — as new urea plants typically take 4–6 years to commission.

4. Price Dynamics: Market Response Analysis

Urea benchmark prices before February 27 were below $500 per metric tonne at the Gulf (FOB basis). Within two weeks of the strikes, prices had crossed $700/metric tonne — a jump of over 40% — driven by a combination of physical supply withdrawal and speculative futures pricing. Argus, a specialist commodities pricing agency, confirmed Middle East urea export prices above $700/tonne as of mid-March 2026.

5. India's Urea Sector: Feedstock Vulnerabilities

India is uniquely exposed to this crisis. The country is the world's second-largest consumer of urea, producing approximately 25 MMT domestically per year (running at ~60% of nameplate capacity) across 30 plants, collectively consuming 75 MMSCMD of natural gas. Domestic gas from the KG Basin covers only ~25% of this requirement; the shortfall is covered by LNG imports, predominantly from Qatar through Hormuz.

On March 3, 2026, Petronet LNG — India's largest gas importer — invoked force majeure, declaring that its LNG vessels could no longer safely transit Hormuz to reach Qatar's Ras Laffan terminal. Notices were served to GAIL, IOCL, and BPCL. GNFC disclosed that its gas allocation was cut to 60% of contracted quantity starting March 6, 2026.

📊 India LNG Import Infrastructure

India's LNG regasification terminals — Dahej (17.5 MMTPA), Hazira (5 MMTPA), and Ennore (5 MMTPA) — are the primary entry points for imported gas feeding urea plants. With Qatar (historically 40% of India's LNG) halted, India pivoted to spot market auctions on March 18, 2026, securing an additional 7–10 MMSCMD and recovering plant utilisation to ~80% of capacity. Emergency imports from Russia (1.4 MMT) and China (2.1 MMT) have been arranged for the 2025–26 fiscal year.

6. Global Cascading Effects: From Farm to Food Price

The crisis is not confined to the Middle East. Its cascading effects are being felt across every major agricultural economy in the world.

United States: USDA AMS reported anhydrous ammonia above $900/tonne and farmers approximately 25% short of fertilizer supplies for the spring planting season. Many corn farmers are switching some acreage to soybeans (a nitrogen-fixing legume), potentially reducing 2026 corn production.

Brazil: Almost entirely reliant on urea imports, with nearly half of these normally transiting Hormuz. With imports disrupted, Brazilian farmers face the worst pre-planting fertilizer shortage since the 2022 Ukraine crisis.

Europe: The European TTF gas benchmark surged 41–63% within weeks, directly increasing the cost of European ammonia and urea manufacturing. Several European producers withdrew price offers within hours of the gas price spike, unable to quote forward contracts. EU was already operating at only ~75% urea production capacity before the conflict, owing to the loss of cheap Russian gas post-2022.

Bangladesh: Among the hardest hit, with four out of five fertilizer factories shut down due to Qatari LNG disruption. Bangladesh's urea import dependence makes it extremely vulnerable to any prolonged disruption.

India's retail urea price is fixed by government subsidy at ₹242 per 45-kg bag, but the global price surge is inflating the government's fertilizer subsidy bill by an estimated ₹45,000 crore additionally. Quality dilution (reducing urea nitrogen content from 46% to 42%) has been reported, and agronomists warn that a 10–15% shortfall in nitrogen application could trigger a 5–7 MMT loss in paddy output, worth approximately ₹50,000 crore in crop value. The UN Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO) projects broader food price inflation of 2–3% globally.

7. Engineering Mitigation Strategies

7.1 Short-Term Operational Responses

Feedstock diversification: Plants with dual-feed capability (LNG + naphtha or LNG + coal) are switching to alternative feedstocks, though at 15–25% higher operating costs. India's FCIL Talcher coal gasification plant (once revived) offers an alternative route — Coal → syngas → NH₃ → Urea — bypassing natural gas entirely.

Nano-urea deployment: IFFCO's Nano Urea liquid (500 ml bottle equivalent to one 45-kg bag) can be applied via foliar spray, reducing surface soil N-requirement by 25–50%. This technology, scaled up rapidly, could partially bridge the supply gap for kharif crops.

Strategic stockpile release: India holds approximately 1.7 MMT of buffer urea stocks. Governments in the US, Brazil, and Europe are similarly releasing strategic reserves to stabilise near-term availability.

7.2 Long-Term Structural Reforms

Green Ammonia via electrolysis: The Haber-Bosch process can theoretically be powered by renewable electricity instead of natural gas — replacing SMR with Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis to produce green hydrogen. India's National Green Hydrogen Mission targets 5 MMT/year of green hydrogen by 2030 with ~12 GW of electrolyser capacity. At current efficiency (50–55 kWh/kg H₂ for PEM electrolysers), scaling to replace conventional NH₃ production would require enormous renewable capacity — but this crisis has dramatically accelerated policy commitment.

// Green Hydrogen Route — PEM Electrolysis
2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂   then   N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
Energy input: 50–55 kWh/kg H₂  |  No CO₂ emissions  |  Green urea = Green NH₃ + CO₂ (from biomass/DAC)  Net: Carbon-neutral urea if powered by renewables

Regional production decentralisation: The Lorenzo Rosa (Carnegie Institution) concept of "Haber-Bosch 2.0" — smaller, modular ammonia plants powered by local renewable energy — would eliminate the geographical mismatch between production (Middle East) and consumption (South Asia, Latin America). Such decentralisation would fundamentally reduce geopolitical supply risk.

Contract diversification: The crisis has exposed the danger of single-source LNG dependency. Engineers and procurement teams must design multi-corridor supply strategies: diversifying across US, Australian, and East African LNG sources, with long-term take-or-pay contracts structured to allow flexible destination clauses during geopolitical disruptions.

✅ The ChE Engineer's Role in Resilience

Chemical engineers must drive the transition to gas-efficient plant designs (targeting <21 GJ/tonne urea, versus today's 22–28 GJ/tonne average), feedstock-flexible reactor systems, and green hydrogen integration. This crisis is not just a geopolitical event — it is a call to redesign the entire nitrogen supply chain architecture with resilience as a primary engineering objective alongside efficiency and cost.

8. Conclusion

The Iran-Israel conflict of 2026 has exposed the extraordinary fragility of the global urea supply chain. The Haber-Bosch process — invented over a century ago, now feeding nearly half of humanity — remains almost entirely dependent on fossil fuel gas, largely produced in a geopolitically volatile region, and shipped through a single 33 km-wide strait. The current crisis is not an anomaly; it is the predictable consequence of a global food system that has prioritised cost optimisation over supply chain resilience for decades.

For chemical engineers, this moment represents both a responsibility and an opportunity. The transition to green ammonia, modular production, and feedstock diversification is no longer merely an environmental aspiration — it is an urgent engineering imperative for global food security. The equations of the Haber-Bosch process have not changed since Fritz Haber first demonstrated them in 1909. What must change, urgently, is the energy source powering them.

As students and future engineers, understanding these supply chain vulnerabilities — thermodynamically, kinetically, and geopolitically — is not an academic exercise. It is the foundational work of building a more resilient world.
